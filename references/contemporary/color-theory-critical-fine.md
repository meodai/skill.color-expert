# Color Theory: A Critical Introduction (Aaron Fine, 2022)

**Author:** Aaron Fine (Professor of Art / Chair, Truman State University; MFA Painting, Claremont)
**Publisher:** Bloomsbury Visual Arts, 2022 (ISBN 9781350027268 / pdf)
**Source:** [Bloomsbury Collections](https://www.bloomsburycollections.com/book/color-theory-a-critical-introduction/) · [Google Books](https://books.google.com/books/about/Color_Theory.html?id=81suEAAAQBAJ)
**PDF:** gitignored (~21 MB); not in repo.

## What It Is

A humanities/critical-theory history of color theory — **not** a technical color-science book. Fine reads the color wheel, the primaries, and "color theory" itself as Western *cultural constructs* loaded with the baggage of colonialism, industrial production, and gender normativity, rather than as empirical facts. The book is a chronological survey (traditional cultures → Greek thought → medieval/Islamic → Newton → Romanticism → classification systems → Bauhaus → postmodern) built as a college textbook, with open-ended "color use activities" throughout.

It explicitly positions itself as extending the critical lineage of **David Batchelor's *Chromophobia*** and **John Gage's *Colour and Culture*** into a comprehensive teaching overview.

## Why This Matters for the Skill

The rest of the knowledge base is overwhelmingly technical (color spaces, perception math, tooling) plus *factual* historical corrections (Moses Harris, Bezold, magenta etymology). This book supplies a register the KB otherwise lacks: **color theory as an ideological/cultural object, and the philosophy of what color *is*.** Three threads here are not covered anywhere else in `references/`.

## Three Genuinely New Threads

### 1. Color adverbialism — Mazviita Chirimuuta, *Outside Color* (2015)

The most substantive idea, and the one with the most reach into the technical material. Chirimuuta argues perceptual science supports a **"chromatic ontology"** located neither in the physical world nor in the mind, but in the *relationship* between perceiver and world (color is a real property of a relation — hence "adverbialism": we see things *colorly*, not "see the colors of things").

- **"Coloring-book hypothesis"** (the orthodox, almost-never-stated view): vision *colors in* an achromatic world, assigning a separable color property to each shape. Black-and-white photography reifies this — it suggests color is an optional layer the mind adds over a "real" achromatic scene.
- **"Coloring-*for*" alternative:** we perceive color *for* shape, motion, depth, material — color is integrated into one perceptual gestalt, not abstracted from it.
- **Payoff — illusions stop being errors.** Adelson's checker-shadow (square A = square B) and simultaneous contrast are *not* failures of a color-detection system; the visual system is correctly using luminance/color to recover 3D shape and lighting. The senses aren't scientific instruments, so there's no "wrong answer." Ties directly to **The Dress** (2015) and **Pascal Wallisch's** account (the brain's prior about light source — daylight vs. incandescent, backlit or not — drives the percept; we don't detect colors then infer lighting, the inference partly *determines* the colors).
- Useful counterweight to the KB's perceptual-uniformity material: it argues *against* treating color as a cleanly separable, measurable object property — the very assumption that OKLCH/CIE engineering is built on.

### 2. The chromophobia thesis (structural, not anecdotal)

Western thought systematically disparages color — coding it as feminine, foreign, superficial, decorative, intoxicating, dangerous, and opposed to *reason / line / drawing / form*. Fine traces it from Greek epistemology (senses as invalid) through Galileo and Locke (color as a "secondary quality," not in the object) to modernist purism. This is an *argument about the field's biases*, distinct from the KB's "the wheel is factually wrong" corrections: here the wheels and primaries are read as **revealing their makers' unspoken priorities and attitudes**, ideological artifacts rather than mistaken physics.

### 3. "Pink is Primary" / contextual color theory

Fine's framing thesis. Using **Berlin & Kay's** basic-color-term research, he argues pink is linguistically irreducible (English speakers reject "light red" as artificial), so any theory adequate to how color *means* must treat pink as primary — something no RYB/CMY wheel can express. Generalizes to a call for **contextual color theory**: the real question is not "how do I mix this green?" but "*what* green, and *why* place it next to that red?" Meaning, not mixing recipe. (Aligns with the skill's existing stance on teaching *why* over *how*.)

Related critical claims worth noting: color theory as a **site where race, sex, and gender are encoded** (the universal light/dark dichotomy mapped onto constructed race); color **standardization** — Munsell → colorimetry → color forecasting — read as an *industrial imperative* rather than neutral progress.

## What Is NOT New Here

The factual history is standard textbook material already well covered: Newton's *Opticks*, the Goethe-vs-Newton "talking past each other" point (Newton describes sub-visible mechanics; Goethe describes experience — they redefine the subject), Munsell, Ostwald, Itten, Albers's *Interaction of Color*, the Bauhaus. **No new color science, spaces, methods, tools, or algorithms.** The originality is the *critical synthesis + pedagogy*, drawn largely from Batchelor, Gage, Chirimuuta, Kuhn, Benjamin, and Wittgenstein's *Remarks on Colour* — not new underlying ideas.

## Practical Takeaways

- When a question is about color *meaning, history, or ideology* (not measurement), this is the register to draw on — color wheels and primaries as cultural choices, not empirical facts.
- Cite **Chirimuuta's adverbialism** when explaining why "illusions" like the checker-shadow or The Dress aren't system failures, and as the philosophical counterpoint to treating color as a separable, measurable object property.
- Cite the **chromophobia** lineage (Fine / Batchelor) when discussing why Western theory privileges line/form over color, or treats bright/excessive color as suspect.
- Primary source to chase next: **Chirimuuta, M. (2015), *Outside Color: Perceptual Science and the Puzzle of Color in Philosophy*, MIT Press** — worth its own reference file.
