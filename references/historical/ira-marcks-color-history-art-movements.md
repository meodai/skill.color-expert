## What this is

A 12-minute **art-historical** survey, not a color-science lesson. Marcks frames the whole
history of Western (and pre-Western) color use through **Johannes Itten's** idea that an
artist's color choices come from a mix of three *intentions*. He then walks that lens forward
through ancient China → Byzantine mosaics → Renaissance → Goethe → Neo-Impressionism →
Impressionism → Expressionism → abstraction. Useful as a narrative spine for "why did artists
use color the way they did," and for the **Goethe allegorical color wheel** and the **John
Gage** quote. The actual color *theory* it teaches (Itten) is better critiqued in
[[itten-seven-contrasts-critical-review]]; the art-historical scholarship behind it is in
[[gage-colour-and-culture]].

## Key idea — Itten's three intentions (the organizing lens)

Marcks attributes to **Johannes Itten** (Swiss expressionist, "maybe the most famous color
theorist of the 20th century") the view that an artist's color aesthetic is informed by some
combination of three types of intention. The video's terms:

| Intention      | Meaning (video's wording)                                  | Itten's canonical term |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| **Impression** | Visual observation — color as the eye records it           | *impressive (optical)* |
| **Expression** | The artist's emotional representation                      | *expressive (emotional)* |
| **Description** | Color's potential for symbolic amplification (meaning)    | *constructive (symbolic)* |

> Note: Itten's published trio is usually rendered *impressive / expressive / constructive*
> (from *The Art of Color* / *The Elements of Color*). Marcks' "description" maps to Itten's
> *constructive*/symbolic intention. The framework itself is a useful descriptive lens but is
> not a scientific account — see [[itten-seven-contrasts-critical-review]] for why Itten's color
> system is widely criticized in color education.

Itten's premise (per the video): there is a "vast difference between color as utility and color
as a gateway to a hidden meaning." Marcks reframes color cognition as three sortable kinds of
experience: **utilitarian** (a stop sign's red), **natural observation** (the sky before a
thunderstorm), and **emotional** (a color that recalls a childhood jacket).

## The art-history walkthrough (color intention over time)

- **Ancient China** — among the earliest civilizations to cultivate a symbolic color language.
  Inspired by the **yellow soil of the Loess Plateau** (cradle of Chinese civilization). Color
  represented the energies of nature; **yellow** sat at the center and was **reserved exclusively
  for the Emperor**. (Impression of the natural world → expression of cultural symbolism.)
- **Byzantine Empire** — mosaic art made to *preserve the stories of sacred saints*. High demand
  for sophistication; subtle variation in color, light, and shadow. Blends imagination,
  impressionistic observation, and symbolic meaning — "the root of so much Western art."
- **Italian Renaissance (15th c.)** — mastered **tonality and color modulation**. Massive, epic,
  hyper-realistic images; symbolism still at the root but stripped of earlier decorative elements.
  "The weight and drama of a scene from a movie."
- **Titian (mid-16th c.)** — the apex of realistic painting *via color*. Used **localized,
  focused color** to give "character and virtue" to the main subjects within crowded compositions
  — a way of bringing symbolism into a realistic style.
- **Goethe (1810)** — see below; the pivot from a Renaissance/academic ideal to an emotional,
  perceptual view of color.
- **Neo-Impressionism (~1880s)** — e.g. **Henri-Edmond Cross**. Deep appreciation for the
  *emotive power of color* plus understanding of optical science. Paintings as "experiments in
  human response to color"; meaning communicated through simplified arrangements of colored dots
  (pointillism / divisionism).
- **Impressionism — Monet** — considered himself "more a student of light than of color."
  Believed **mixing pigments breaks the power of pure color**, and that the eye's urge for visual
  closure lets the viewer reassemble broken color into meaningful scenes (optical mixing).
- **Cézanne** — expanded the Renaissance ideal of **color modulation** by fusing it with
  Impressionism: representational scenes that "glow with exaggerated color effects," layering
  subtle rhythms of cool and warm to heighten reality.
- **Expressionism (turn of the century)** — e.g. **Edvard Munch**: color as a vehicle for
  *internal psychology*. "Nature is not only all that is visible to the eye; it also includes the
  inner pictures of the soul."
- **Hilma af Klint** — among the earliest to break from representational color. "Light was the
  first phenomenon of the world." Built a personal color language — **golden yellow = masculine
  energy, eggshell blue = feminine energy** — to explore human energies in harmony with nature.
- **Piet Mondrian** — "a theoretician as much as a painter." Abstracted everything down to
  **primary colors intentionally arranged** to reach the "fundamental quality of objects" and
  connect with a deeper part of the human relationship to form and color.

Closing thesis: *"The story of color is really the story of creative intention."* In any color
image you can read impression, expression, and symbolism reflecting the artist's relationship
with their world.

## Goethe's *Theory of Colours* (1810) — the emotional/allegorical color wheel

**Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** published *Zur Farbenlehre* (*Theory of Colours*) in 1810. An
author of emotional literature with an equal interest in visual art and science, he meant the
book to **change the world's perception of color**. Goethe sought to validate the *scientific
function of human imagination* in response to light and color — to him the warmth of the setting
sun and the coolness of the Earth's rising shadow were evidence not just of white light
fracturing but of *humanity's poetic intuition*.

Goethe's wheel looks superficially like earlier rational color wheels, but he assigned
**allegorical qualities** to each field:

| Hue        | Allegorical quality (Goethe, per video) |
| ---------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Red**    | Beauty                                  |
| **Orange** | Nobility                                |
| **Yellow** | Good                                    |
| **Green**  | Usefulness                              |
| **Blue**   | Commonality                             |
| **Violet** | The unnecessary / spiritual             |

The scientific establishment found **no academic value in Goethe's optics** (his anti-Newtonian
stance was wrong on the physics), but his *studies of color* — the perceptual and psychological
observations — remained influential to this day, and resonated well beyond artists.

> Goethe's primary text is downloaded in `pdfs/goethe-theory-of-colours-1840.pdf` (Eastlake's
> 1840 English translation, public domain). Goethe's empirical observations on edge colors and
> colored shadows underpin the modern "Goethe edge colors as a design hack" technique — see the
> techniques folder.

### The John Gage quote (color historian)

Marcks credits art historian **John Gage** (here mislabeled "technologist"; Gage is the author
of *Colour and Culture* — see [[gage-colour-and-culture]]) with this summary of Goethe:

> "Goethe argued there is no color in the physical world — there are only patterns of light and
> dark, and these patterns are a sensation produced by our very souls."

Marcks calls the connection between **poetic intuition and color science** "a major aspect of
every modern art movement."

## How to use this in practice

- **As a teaching narrative**, not a system: the impression/expression/symbolism lens is a clean
  way to *talk about why a palette feels the way it does*, without claiming predictive power.
  Pair it with the perceptual realities of [[itten-seven-contrasts-critical-review]] so you
  don't repeat Itten's overreaches.
- **Symbolism is culture-bound**: yellow = imperial power (China) vs. yellow = "good" (Goethe)
  shows the same hue carrying opposite-register meanings across cultures. Don't treat color–emotion
  mappings as universal.
- **Optical mixing > pigment mixing for vibrancy** (Monet / Neo-Impressionists): keeping pure
  hues adjacent and letting the eye blend them preserves chroma that physically mixing pigments
  destroys — the same principle behind perceptual interpolation and why RGB averaging muddies
  color.
